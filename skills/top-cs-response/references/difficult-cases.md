# Difficult cases

Provenance: `conservative-implementation`; patterns are `synthetic-example`.

## Impossible or design-incompatible experiment

1. Acknowledge what claim the experiment would test.
2. Explain the real design dependency: unavailable longitudinal follow-up, new population, different intervention, inaccessible oracle, or incompatible system.
3. Offer supplied alternative evidence without pretending equivalence.
4. Narrow the affected claim and add a concrete limitation.
5. Mark unresolved evidence explicitly.

Do not call an experiment impossible merely because it is expensive or inconvenient.

## Reviewer factual error

Verify the record first. Restate the issue, provide exact manuscript/equation/figure evidence, explain the likely ambiguity in the manuscript, and clarify the text. Do not accuse the reviewer or speculate about expertise.

## Conflicting reviewer requests

Preserve both IDs and any editor instruction. Identify the shared objective and incompatible actions. Choose the action consistent with evidence and editor priority, then explain the same scientific boundary to each reviewer without making contradictory promises.

## Reviewer-requested citation

Check identity, relevance, and relationship to the exact claim. If verified, add and position it fairly. If irrelevant, explain the scope distinction. If metadata or content is unavailable, use `AUTHOR_INPUT_NEEDED`; never invent bibliographic details.

## Major statistical critique

Separate reporting omissions from invalid design. Request experimental unit, sample/run count, test, assumptions, effect, uncertainty, multiple-comparison correction, and code/output. If the analysis cannot support the claim, soften it rather than writing a confident response around missing statistics.

## Baseline fairness dispute

Record information access, data, preprocessing, compute, tuning, stopping, and selection for each method. If parity cannot be established, present the comparison as conditional or add a matched analysis. Do not dismiss the concern with `we followed prior work` alone.

## Ethics, compliance, or data-integrity concern

Do not use persuasive language to conceal a real problem. Inventory records and governing policy, flag missing approvals/licenses/consent/provenance, avoid claiming compliance without documentation, and escalate to the human authors. Mark package readiness `blocked` when truthful submission is not possible.

## Transfer after review

Separate reusable scientific responses from destination-specific policy and framing. Preserve prior reviewer IDs, but do not represent transferred comments as new reviewers or assume the new venue accepts the same revision package.

## Appeal-like request

Distinguish procedural error from disagreement with scientific judgment. Require the governing appeal policy, decision record, and specific procedural basis. Keep routine rebuttal drafting separate. Do not promise reversal or characterize disagreement as misconduct.

## Reviewer silence or unresolved follow-up

Preserve the last answered ID and unresolved status. Do not infer agreement from silence. A later meta-review response should summarize decision-critical unresolved evidence without repeating the full initial rebuttal.
